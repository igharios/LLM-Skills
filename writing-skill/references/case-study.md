# Case Study Framework

**Audience:** Anyone in software engineering — practitioners, skeptics, evaluators
**Length:** 3–5 pages (~1,500–2,500 words)
**Voice:** Narrative, evidence-heavy, specific — let the results do the talking
**Published on:** senzostack.com or client site

---

## Structure

Section headers use ALL CAPS — this is a SenzoStack/HumanLogic structural signature.

### INDUSTRY
- One line. Name the domain precisely.
- Example: "AI-driven agile delivery / product-team productivity tools"

### SUMMARY
- 1 tight paragraph: who the client is, what they're building, why it matters
- Enough context to orient any software engineering reader
- No jargon that requires prior knowledge of the client

### ENGAGEMENT
- Describe the scope and timeline of the work
- What was the starting point? What was HumanLogic/SenzoStack hired to do?
- 2–3 paragraphs
- **Include a client quote here as a blockquote** — this is mandatory
  - Quote should speak to the quality of the working relationship and the outcome
  - Attributed to: Name, Title, Company

### THE PROBLEM
- State the problem sharply. Not vague pain — a specific, named constraint with stakes attached.
- Bold the key tension or insight within the problem statement
- Example: "Agile development coaches currently spend **60% of their time on administrative
  overhead**... Meanwhile, the team dynamics that actually determine delivery outcomes go
  unaddressed. This isn't a coaching problem. It's a **design problem.**"

### BACKGROUND
- What existed before the engagement
- What was already known or tried
- Why the standard approaches weren't working
- Sets up the approach section — the reader should arrive here understanding why something
  had to change

### PROCESS
- What was actually done, step by step
- Specific enough to be credible — name the methods, tools, and decisions
- Use bullet points for parallel process items (workshops, interviews, frameworks)
- Include visuals where they exist: personas, journey maps, architecture diagrams, frameworks
- Highlight the moment(s) where the approach was non-obvious or harder than expected

### BUSINESS RESULTS
- Measurable outcomes first, then qualitative
- Each result as a bolded outcome + explanation:
  `**[What changed]:** [Why it matters / what it enabled]`
- Minimum 3–5 specific results
- Avoid vague outcomes: not "improved team efficiency" — name the specific change
- Close with transferable lesson: what does this tell someone at a different company
  with a similar problem?

### FOR MORE INFORMATION
- CTA to contact or learn more
- Website + email

---

## Must Include
- Client quote in the ENGAGEMENT section (blockquote format)
- At least 2–3 specific, named outcomes with measurable detail where possible
- A moment in PROCESS where the approach was non-obvious
- Transferable lesson at the close of BUSINESS RESULTS — this is what makes
  a case study worth reading even if you're not the client

## Must Avoid
- Happy-path-only narrative — readers don't trust case studies that have no friction
- Generic process descriptions that could apply to any engagement
- Burying the result — key outcomes should not appear only at the very end
- Outcome language like "improved", "enhanced", "optimized" without specifics

## SenzoStack / HumanLogic Case Study Style Patterns

Observed from humanlogic.com/blog/2026/04/19/transforming-ai-capability-into-agile-coaching-impact:

**Section headers:** ALL CAPS, bold
**Client quote:** Long-form blockquote in ENGAGEMENT section, full attribution
**Problem statement:** Short, punchy, ends with a reframe ("This isn't X. It's Y.")
**Process:** Mix of prose and bullets. Visual artifacts referenced inline.
**Results:** Bold lead-in per result, explanation follows. Mix of strategic and tactical outcomes.
**Closing:** Zooms out to the universal lesson before the CTA.

**Tone markers:**
- "This isn't a [X] problem. It's a [Y] problem." — reframe pattern used in THE PROBLEM
- "Rather than X, they Y" — contrast structure used in PROCESS
- Results stated as capability gains, not just activity completions
