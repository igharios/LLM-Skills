# Blog Post Framework

**Audience:** Executives and engineering leaders
**Length:** 1–1.5 pages (~600–900 words)
**Voice:** Direct, opinionated, practitioner-aware — no fluff
**Published on:** senzostack.com/blog and/or agenticproductdelivery.substack.com

---

## Structure

Structure is principled, not rigid. The order below reflects natural argument flow —
adjust when the content calls for it, but never skip a component.

### 1. Title + Subtitle
- Title makes a claim or surfaces a tension. Not a topic. Not a question.
- Subtitle is one line that frames the piece. Think of it as the thesis in plain English.
- Examples:
  - ✅ "From agile to Agile to AgIle" / "We use the word Agile a lot. Here's why it's struggling."
  - ✅ "Context: The Missing Ingredient in Team Intelligence"
  - ❌ "AI and Agile: What You Need to Know"

### 2. Featured Image
- Always included. Referenced at the top of the post.
- Use `/imagegen` skill to generate if one doesn't exist.

### 3. Hook (Opening paragraph)
- A sharp observation, uncomfortable truth, or pattern the reader hasn't named yet.
- Not a definition. Not background. Not "in this post I will..."
- Should make an engineering leader think "yes, that's exactly it" or "I've never thought of it that way."
- 2–3 sentences max.

### 4. The Real Problem
- Reframe what the reader thinks the problem is.
- This is where the post's point of view lives. Say the thing that's true but uncomfortable.
- One tight paragraph.

### 5. The Insight
- One clear, defensible idea. Not a list of tips. Not "here are three ways..."
- Develop the argument. Use a concrete example, a named pattern, or a real scenario (anonymized if needed).
- This is the body of the post — can be 2–3 paragraphs but stays focused on the single insight.

### 6. So What
- Practical implication for an exec or engineering leader.
- Not generic advice. What does this insight change about how they think or what they do?
- One paragraph.

### 7. Close
- Don't tie it up. Leave a question, a provocation, or a tension that stays with the reader.
- The best closes make the reader want to talk about it.
- 1–3 sentences.

### 8. Author Byline
- "By [Name]" and co-author if applicable.
- Tags: relevant topic labels (agile, ai-agents, delivery, etc.)

---

## Must Include
- A single strong point of view — not "on one hand / on the other hand" writing
- At least one concrete example or named pattern from the field
- Language that signals practitioner experience, not observer commentary

## Must Avoid
- Generic AI-sounding intros
- Listicles dressed as insight ("5 ways to improve...")
- Endings that summarize what was just said
- Explaining basics the audience already knows
- Passive voice burying the argument

## SenzoStack Blog Style Patterns

Observed from senzostack.com/blog:

- Posts open mid-thought — the reader is dropped into the argument, not introduced to it
- Section headers are short and descriptive (H2 only, H3 for sub-points)
- Bold used sparingly for key phrases that carry the argument
- No callout boxes, no sidebars — clean single column
- Posts feel written by a practitioner, not a content team
- Sentences like "The coaching frame was too small for what we were building. So we changed the name." —
  short, confident, no excess words
