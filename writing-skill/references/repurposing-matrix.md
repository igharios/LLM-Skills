# Repurposing Matrix

When a Tier 1 asset (blog, whitepaper, case study) is being promoted via a Tier 2
distribution format (LinkedIn post, newsletter), use this file alongside the target
format's reference file.

**Always read both:** the target format reference (linkedin.md or newsletter.md)
AND this file before writing repurposed content.

---

## Cross-Cutting Rules

These apply to every repurposing pattern without exception.

1. **Extract, don't summarize.**
   A summary tells them what's in it. An extract gives them something *from* it.
   One makes the reader feel briefed. The other makes them want more.

2. **One insight per distribution piece.**
   The temptation to pack everything in kills the tease. Pick one and make it land.

3. **The distribution piece must work without the click.**
   If it only makes sense after reading the source, it's a summary, not a tease.

4. **The insight must differ from the headline.**
   If the reader gets the same thing from the title alone, there's no reason to click.

5. **LinkedIn CTA always goes in comments, not the post body.**
   LinkedIn suppresses reach on posts with external links in the body.

## The 6 Patterns

---

### Blog → LinkedIn Post

**What to extract:**
The single sharpest claim from the blog — the one sentence that would make an engineering
leader stop scrolling. Usually not the conclusion. Often buried in the middle of the post.

**How to tease:**
- BLUF states the claim directly (don't save it for the end)
- Post argues the point with one concrete example
- Don't retell the blog — make the point land independently
- The blog goes deeper, but the post should be complete enough to stand alone

**CTA:**
Last paragraph earns the link naturally.
- ✅ "Wrote this out in full — link in comments."
- ❌ "Read more here →"

---

### Blog → Newsletter (Publications & Events section)

**What to extract:**
The insight most relevant to an engineering leader reader — may not be the blog's
central thesis. Choose based on what will land for a subscriber.

**How to tease:**
3–4 sentences:
1. What the blog argues (one sentence)
2. The specific insight most relevant to the newsletter audience
3. Who should read it (be specific — "engineering leaders dealing with X", not "everyone")
4. Link as CTA button: "Read the post →"

---

### Whitepaper → LinkedIn Post

**What to extract:**
One of the numbered insights from the whitepaper — specifically the most counterintuitive
or uncomfortable one. Not the summary. Not the intro thesis.

**How to tease:**
- BLUF states the position from that one insight
- Two paragraphs develop the argument
- Final paragraph names the tension the whitepaper resolves
- Example close: "Full analysis with risk factors and the path forward in the whitepaper — link in comments."

**Rule:** Never compress all insights into one post. One insight done well beats
three done weakly.

---

### Whitepaper → Newsletter (Publications & Events section)

**What to extract:**
The insight that will land hardest with a delivery or engineering manager.
Often the risks/counterweights section — the most honest part, and the one
that signals intellectual credibility.

**How to tease:**
3–4 sentences:
1. Name the whitepaper's central argument
2. Surface the insight that challenges conventional thinking
3. Specify the audience: "for engineering managers navigating X" — not generic
4. Link as CTA button: "Read the whitepaper →"

**Rule:** The insight teased must be different from the whitepaper headline.

---

### Case Study → LinkedIn Post

**What to extract:**
The transferable lesson — not the story, not the client details, not the process steps.
The thing someone at a different company with a similar problem can use today.

**How to tease:**
- BLUF leads with the outcome or the non-obvious lesson (not the client name)
- Post makes the point. Case study proves it. But the post stands alone.
- Don't summarize the story — extract the insight the story unlocks
- Example BLUF: "Coaching time reclaimed isn't a productivity metric. It's a design outcome."

**CTA:**
- ✅ "Full case study with process, personas, and results — link in comments."
- ❌ "We published a new case study — check it out!"

---

### Case Study → Newsletter (Publications & Events section)

**What to extract:**
The result that matters most to the reader — the business outcome, not process detail.
Specificity is credibility: name the metric, the decision that changed, or the capability gained.

**How to tease:**
3–4 sentences:
1. Client profile: who they are and what problem they faced (company name or anonymized profile)
2. The approach in one sentence
3. The result that matters most — specific, named
4. Why it's relevant to a software engineering reader
5. Link as CTA button: "Read the case study →"

---

## Decision Guide

When a user asks to "announce" or "promote" a Tier 1 asset:

```
1. Identify source asset type → blog / whitepaper / case study
2. Identify target channel(s) → LinkedIn / newsletter / both
3. Read target format reference file (linkedin.md or newsletter.md)
4. Read this file for the specific extraction and teasing pattern
5. Write the distribution piece — extract one insight, tease it, earn the click
```

When a user provides the source asset text:
- Read it before writing. Identify the sharpest insight that fits the pattern above.
- Don't default to the intro or the conclusion — those are usually the weakest extract points.
- The best extract is often a specific example, a risk named plainly, or a reframe buried mid-document.
